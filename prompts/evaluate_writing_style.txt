# Evaluation Dimensions with Specific Similarity Scales

## 1. **Readability & Jargon Level**
Compare the reading level and amount of legal jargon vs. plain language. Consider technical terminology density and accessibility to non-legal readers.

**Similarity Scale:**
- 5 = Nearly identical reading level and jargon density; same balance of technical/plain language throughout
- 4 = Very similar complexity with minor differences in terminology choices or occasional variance in technical language use
- 3 = Moderate differences in accessibility; one summary noticeably more technical in some sections but overall similar approach
- 2 = Significantly different approaches to language complexity; one consistently more technical or accessible than the other
- 1 = Completely different target audiences (e.g., one highly technical for legal professionals, other simplified for general public)

## 2. **Narrative Order**
Compare whether events are presented in the same sequence. Consider chronological vs. thematic organization and the order of key facts and arguments.

**Similarity Scale:**
- 5 = Identical sequence of information presentation; same events, facts, and arguments in same order
- 4 = Same overall flow with 1-2 elements reordered; core narrative structure preserved
- 3 = Similar general structure but several sections in different order; recognizable but rearranged
- 2 = Different organizational approaches with some overlap; mix of chronological and thematic that diverge
- 1 = Completely different information architecture (e.g., one chronological, other organized by legal issues)

## 3. **Sentence Structure & Voice**
Compare diversity of sentence types, active vs. passive voice balance, and verb-tense consistency.

**Similarity Scale:**
- 5 = Nearly identical sentence patterns, voice usage, and tense choices throughout
- 4 = Very similar writing style with occasional differences in sentence complexity or voice
- 3 = Moderate variation; one tends toward longer/shorter sentences or more active/passive constructions
- 2 = Noticeably different writing styles; consistent differences in sentence variety and voice preferences
- 1 = Completely different approaches (e.g., one uses varied, active sentences; other uses uniform, passive constructions)

## 4. **Formatting & Layout**
Compare use of headings, bullet lists, numbered lists, paragraphing, and other visible structure cues.

**Similarity Scale:**
- 5 = Identical formatting choices; same use of headings, lists, and paragraph breaks
- 4 = Very similar structure with minor variations (e.g., one additional heading or slightly different list formatting)
- 3 = Similar formatting approach but noticeable differences in execution (e.g., both use headings but different levels/frequency)
- 2 = Different formatting philosophies; one significantly more structured than the other
- 1 = Completely different approaches (e.g., one heavily formatted with multiple levels; other is continuous prose)

## 5. **Citation & Reference Style**
Compare presence, position, and formatting of case/statute citations or footnotes. Consider inline vs. separate citations, citation density, and formatting conventions.

**Similarity Scale:**
- 5 = Identical citation approach; same style, frequency, and positioning of references
- 4 = Very similar citation practices with minor formatting differences or occasional variation in placement
- 3 = Similar citation philosophy but different execution (e.g., both cite cases but different density or positioning)
- 2 = Different citation approaches; one significantly more reference-heavy or uses different citation style
- 1 = Completely different or incomparable (e.g., one has extensive citations, other has none; or entirely different citation formats)
